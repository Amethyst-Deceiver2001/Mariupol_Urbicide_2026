# The Archons' House
### Mariupol: siege, occupation, and the bureaucratic seizure of a city

*Narrative preamble to the Mariupol Urbicide Project master exhibit. Establishes
the historical record, the conceptual frame, and the architecture of dispossession
the rest of this document evidences property by property.*

---

## I. The city before

Before 24 February 2022, Mariupol was a port city of roughly 425,000–450,000 people
on the Sea of Azov: iron and steel works, a deep-water harbour, a university, music
and art festivals, and an iconic drama theatre. It was ordinary in the way cities
are ordinary — the bus you took to work, the bedroom you grew up in, the courtyard
where children played. That ordinariness is worth holding onto, because what follows
is the story of an administrative machine built to make it disappear from the record.

## II. The siege

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine began on 24 February 2022, and the assault on
Mariupol began the same day. Within days the city was encircled. By 2 March the entire
city had lost power, water, and sanitation; hundreds of thousands of residents were
trapped for weeks in basements without running water, electricity, heat, telephone, or
any reliable news of what was happening above them.

The siege lasted nearly three months and produced a catalogue of attacks on civilian
life that became internationally synonymous with the word *atrocity*. On 9 March a
strike hit a children's and maternity hospital. On 16 March the Donetsk Academic
Regional Drama Theatre — the city's main bomb shelter, with the word **ДЕТИ**
("CHILDREN") painted in letters large enough to read from orbit on the pavement at
either end — was bombed; estimates of the dead there run from a dozen to roughly six
hundred. Residents buried their dead in courtyards and roadside graves. Evacuation
corridors were repeatedly agreed and then broke down; those who fled by car threaded
dozens of checkpoints where they were searched, interrogated, and sometimes detained.

The human toll has never been fully counted, and the range of estimates is itself part
of the record. The UN human rights office verified roughly 1,348 individual civilian
deaths directly in the hostilities — while cautioning that access constraints made
this a floor, not a total. Human Rights Watch, analysing satellite imagery of grave
sites, estimated more than 8,000 dead. Ukrainian officials have put the figure at
25,000 or more; Mstyslav Chernov, whose footage became *20 Days in Mariupol*,
estimates 70,000–80,000. The United Nations assessed that up to **90 percent of
Mariupol's multistorey residential buildings** were damaged or destroyed, along with
some 60 percent of private houses.

Russia assumed effective control of the city in May 2022, after the surrender of the
last defenders at the Azovstal steelworks. Of the half-million who had lived there, an
estimated 350,000–400,000 had fled; perhaps 150,000 remained by May, gradually
regaining basic services only in the second half of the year. The displaced are now
scattered across Ukraine, Russia, and the wider world — and they are the dispossessed
owners at the centre of everything that follows.

## III. The integration

What happened next was not reconstruction in any ordinary sense. It was a change of
sovereign, imposed by law.

On 30 September 2022, Russia declared the annexation of the Donetsk region (among
others), formalised by federal constitutional law **ФКЗ №5-ФКЗ of 4 October 2022**,
which made Russian civil, land, and housing law applicable in Mariupol. The ruble, the
Russian passport, the Russian school curriculum, and the Russian property registry
followed. The city was renamed into a Russian administrative object, its streets
re-lettered, its cadastre re-drawn, its residents re-classified as people who must now
prove, under Russian rules, that what was theirs is still theirs.

Crucially, the legal machinery did not wait for the legal annexation. The master
predicate for the entire demolish-rebuild-reallocate programme — Постановление ГКО ДНР
№1 — was signed on **6 April 2022**, three days after the occupation "State Defence
Committee" that issued it was itself created, and nearly six months *before* the
federal law that supposedly authorised everything. The citywide master-plan decree
(Указ №420) followed on 30 July 2022; the demolition procedure (ГКО №162) on 23 July
2022. The paperwork of seizure was being drafted while the city was still smouldering
and the dead were still in the courtyards. Whatever else can be debated, the *sequence*
forecloses any reading of this as improvised or reactive. The machine was designed in
advance.

## IV. The archons' house

It is worth pausing on what kind of machine it is, because its weapon is not the
airstrike but the *record*.

In *Archive Fever* (1995), Jacques Derrida traces the word "archive" to the Greek
*arkheion* — the house of the *archons*, the magistrates who commanded. The archive
begins, he notes, as a private residence: the place where those with power keep the
official documents, and from which they exercise not only custody but interpretation.
The archons are "at once" the guardians of the record and the ones with "the
hermeneutic right and competence" to make the documents *speak the law*. The Greek root
*arkhē*, he writes, names "at once the commencement and the commandment" — origin and
authority in a single word. To hold the archive is to hold the power to decide what is
recorded, what is recognised, and what is allowed to disappear.

This is a precise description of what the occupation has built in Mariupol. It has
seized the archons' house. It has made itself the sole keeper *and* interpreter of the
record of who owns what — and under the December 2025 federal amendment, **inclusion in
the registry is title**. The entry in the list now *is* the law it speaks.

Derrida pairs this archontic power with its shadow: a destructive drive within
archiving itself — the "archiviolithic," the impulse that "operates in silence" and
"never leaves any archives of its own." The Mariupol record shows both faces at once.
The same apparatus that *inscribes* — 12,948 flats entered into an "ownerless" registry,
2,666 court transfers, hundreds of land grants — also *erases*: it demolishes the
building, renames the street, and breaks the address so that the destroyed property,
on paper, no longer exists. The demolition list that ordered the clearing of one
documented building (Распоряжение ГКО №56) is, fittingly, absent from every public
register — an act of erasure that, as Derrida would predict, leaves no archive of
itself.

Two further frames sharpen the point. Hannah Arendt's analysis of statelessness — the
condition of those who have lost "the right to have rights," made superfluous not by a
single cruelty but by the quiet operation of a bureaucracy that no longer counts them —
describes exactly the displaced Mariupol owner, alive and identifiable, whose flat is
nonetheless ruled "ownerless." And James C. Scott's account of *legibility* — the
modern state's drive to remake territory and people into forms it can read and tax and
own, through cadastres, standardised names, and registration — describes the
re-addressing, re-cadastring, and re-registration of the city with uncomfortable
precision. Dispossession here is not lawlessness. It is an excess of a certain kind of
law: the law of the register, turned against the people it counts.

## V. The architecture of exclusion

Read across its domains, the dispossession is systematic, and its design has a single
discernible purpose: to transfer property out of the hands of the displaced and into
the occupier's market and population, while wrapping each step in the form of a legal
procedure. The scaffolding is built from requirements that are individually citable as
"rules" and collectively impossible for a displaced Ukrainian to satisfy.

- **Manufactured ownerlessness.** A war-emptied flat whose owner has fled is declared a
  *бесхозяйная недвижимая вещь* — an "ownerless thing" — under Russian civil-code
  article 225 as imported into the occupation's decrees. Non-payment of utilities (in a
  city whose utilities Russia itself cut) and absence from a Russian registry are
  treated as evidence of abandonment.

- **Unrealistic timeframes.** Owners are given short, published windows — on the order
  of thirty days — to come forward and contest a designation, with notice posted to an
  occupation website and a building entrance the owner cannot visit. The master
  re-registration deadline has been pulled *forward* to **1 July 2026**, compressing the
  window further.

- **A demand for physical presence.** To assert a pre-existing right, the owner must, in
  effect, appear — in person, in occupied territory, before occupation authorities — to
  produce documents and re-register. For someone displaced to Ukrainian-held territory
  or abroad, this is not a high bar; it is a closed door.

- **Filtration.** Entry into the occupied zone runs through "filtration": interrogation,
  biometric and device screening, and entry bans. Tens of thousands have been denied
  entry, many with bans of 20–50 years. The same state that requires the owner to appear
  in person is the state that bars the owner from entering — a contradiction that is not
  a bug but the mechanism.

- **Citizenship as a gate.** Re-registration, compensation, and the subsidised mortgages
  that move seized stock to new buyers are structured around Russian citizenship and a
  Russian passport — documents the displaced owner cannot and will not hold, and which
  the new, incoming buyer holds by default.

Each rung — ownerless designation, court transfer, demolition, land reallocation,
rebuild at a new address, resale, parallel allocation of seized housing to officials and
military, and the renaming that severs the address itself — is documented in the
occupation's own published records. Taken together they describe not a series of
discretionary hardships but an integrated pipeline whose output is the lawful-looking
transfer of a city's housing stock to the occupying power's population.

The scaffolding's individual elements are corroborated, point for point, by the
occupation's own promotional media. In a November 2023 RIA Novosti feature on Mariupol's
property market, state journalist Regina Orekhova films estate agents who explain — as
selling points — that the subsidised mortgage is pegged to a salary the locals cannot
earn; that a flat rebuilt on a demolished footprint carries "a different address, so
there's no claim to compensation"; and, flatly, that "we locals can't afford these — it's
just Russians from Russia buying them." The harm this project documents from the decrees
is narrated aloud, a year earlier, by the apparatus that profits from it. Even the
vernacular gives the game away: the agent calls a bombed-out flat a *razrushka* — the
destruction-root *razrushit'* dressed in the affectionate diminutive that turns
*grandfather* into *gramps*. A destroyed home, made to sound like an opportunity.

## VI. Not an aberration but a method

It would be a mistake to read this as the improvisation of a single wartime
administration. The technique was rehearsed. After 2014, occupied Crimea served as the
laboratory: a decade of "nationalisation" decrees culminating in a 2020 Putin decree
barring "foreigners" from owning property, which cut foreign-owned plots from 13,859 to
roughly 5,500. The instruments tested there — designation, citizenship gates, in-person
requirements, court laundering — were then applied across every territory seized since
2022. By August 2025 Russia's own registrar, Rosreestr, recorded on the order of 550,000
properties taken as "ownerless" across occupied Ukraine. Mariupol's twelve thousand are
one city's share of a national programme; this project resolves that programme, for one
city, down to the named property, decree, judge, and beneficiary.

The technique also has a name in the scholarship the project takes its own name from.
*Urbicide* — the deliberate killing of a city — was theorised after the 1992–95 Bosnian
war to capture destruction of the built environment as a distinct form of violence, one
aimed at annihilating the plural, heterogeneous community a city makes possible. Mariupol
extends the concept: the city was first killed by bombardment (93 percent of one central
district's high-rises damaged) and is now being killed a second time, bureaucratically —
its displaced struck from the property rolls, its streets renamed, its survivors offered
their neighbours' confiscated flats while the new-builds are sold to incomers. Destruction
by ordnance, then erasure by registry.

Property is only one limb of a wider, codified de-Ukrainianisation programme, and the
other limbs share the same paper-trail structure this project documents for housing. An
education-ministry order (No. 467, 18 June 2025) made "Mother Tongue" and "Literature in
the Mother Tongue" elective from 1 September 2025 — the administrative mechanism by which
Ukrainian language instruction is removed from occupied schools; the UK Ministry of
Defence has described the wider campaign as one to "extirpate Ukrainian culture, identity,
and statehood." The toponymy layer this project tracks (rung [H]) is one piece of the same
apparatus, run in reverse: Ukraine itself renamed over 51,000 toponymic objects during
2014–2021 decommunisation; the occupation is now re-imposing Soviet and Russian names
street by street. Ukraine's Human Rights Commissioner, Dmytro Lubinets, has called it
plainly "a total system. Streets are renamed, cultural and architectural monuments are
demolished… they tear off all symbols of statehood." Read together with the property
seizure documented here, language removal and toponymy inversion are the same
population-transfer apparatus working on a different register — and they corroborate,
rather than compete with, the Rome Statute 8(2)(b)(viii) argument this project makes from
the housing data alone.

None of which, in law, transfers a single title. The governing precedent is settled, and
it has now been applied directly to this war. In *Loizidou v. Turkey* (1996), the European
Court of Human Rights held that an unrecognised occupation regime cannot validly
expropriate property: the refugee barred from her home in occupied northern Cyprus
*remained its lawful owner*, the occupier's expropriation law was void, and the denial of
access was a *continuing violation* — a wrong that does not expire and for which
reparation is owed. On 9 July 2025, the Grand Chamber's judgment in *Ukraine and the
Netherlands v. Russia* — consolidating Russia's conduct in Crimea, Donbas, and the
full-scale invasion into a single inter-state case — applied that same principle to these
exact territories: the Court found Russia responsible for a *systematic administrative
practice* of Article 1 Protocol 1 violations, expropriating civilian property "without
compensation or access to impartial remedy," and characterised the violations as "a
coherent strategy rather than isolated incidents." Loizidou supplied the principle; this
is the Court ruling, on this war, that the principle holds. That is the precise legal
ground beneath this entire archive. Every occupation registry entry, court ruling, and
land grant catalogued here is evidence of an ongoing crime, not proof of a new owner. The
displaced Mariupol family still holds title; what the project documents is the system
built to pretend otherwise.

The residential seizure this project documents also has a corporate twin already being
tested in court, on the identical legal theory. Metinvest filed more than sixteen claims
at the ECtHR (Oct 2022) over the occupation's appropriation of the Azovstal and Ilyich
steelworks; Rinat Akhmetov's SCM filed a bilateral-investment-treaty claim (2023) seeking
$17–20bn, arguing the DNR and LNR administrations have been "acting under Russian
direction or control since 2014" — the same attribution chain a residential claim depends
on. Sophisticated corporate litigants are already pressing, with extensive legal
resources, the very theory this project assembles evidence for at the level of a single
flat: that the occupying power is liable for what its proxy administration signs.

## VII. The record turned around

This is where the project begins. The occupation built an archive to launder the
seizure; the same archive is the evidence of it. Every "ownerless" designation names a
property. Every court ruling names a date, a judge, and an enabling statute. Every land
grant names a beneficiary. Every demolition register, every re-addressed new build,
every resale listing is a dated, self-incriminating artifact produced by the seizing
authority itself.

The Mariupol Urbicide Project captures that record — forensically, at scale, and with a
documented chain of custody (SHA-256 hash and UTC timestamp on every artifact, Berkeley
Protocol throughout) — and reassembles it onto a single evidentiary spine: which
property, owned by whom, was taken how, by whom, when, and to whose benefit. The
structured output is built to feed two endpoints: **restitution**, through the Council
of Europe's Register of Damage for Ukraine, and **criminal accountability**, under the
Rome Statute — unlawful appropriation of property and the transfer of the occupier's own
population into occupied territory.

The exhibits that follow move from the system to the specific: the legal pipeline and
its instruments; the network of actors who operate it, from the apex signer down to the
shell developers; and two worked cases — a single demolished building rebuilt under a
new address and sold, and three standing buildings whose flats were processed one by one
through the registry and are being advertised for sale right now. What unites them is
the thing Derrida saw in the word itself: whoever holds the archive holds the power to
say what is and was. This project is an attempt to hold the record open — to keep, against
an apparatus built to forget, an account of what was taken and from whom.
