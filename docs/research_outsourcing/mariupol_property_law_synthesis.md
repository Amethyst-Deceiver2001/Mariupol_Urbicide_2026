# Mariupol Property Dispossession: Russian Property-Law Evolution, Ukrainian Multi-Family Ownership, and Internal Legal Contradictions

## Overview

The findings synthesized here show that mass housing dispossession in occupied Mariupol became possible through a layered legal strategy rather than a single decree.[cite:254][cite:259][cite:221] Russia first normalized discriminatory treatment of Ukrainian owners in occupied Crimea through foreign-ownership and borderland rules, then entrenched the idea that only rights registered in the Russian EGRN count as legally cognizable, then extended those concepts to the occupied mainland through federal constitutional integration and a 2025 federal law on housing with “signs of ownerless property.”[cite:258][cite:266][cite:235][cite:259] Municipal bodies in Mariupol, especially the property-and-land administration at Chernomorsky 10 and the housing administration on Shevchenko Boulevard, operationalize those federal and regional rules at the address level.[cite:222][cite:231][cite:221]

At the same time, Ukrainian housing law is structured around a very different model: apartment owners in multi-family buildings are co-owners of the building’s common property and, in principle, of the land plot and adjoining territory under and around the building, with those rights surviving destruction of the building itself.[cite:251][cite:247][cite:253] The Russian occupation regime replaces that model with a system that privileges Russian citizenship, Russian registration, Russian physical presence, and Russian administrative discretion.[cite:221][cite:228][cite:235] Even within Russia’s own framework, there is evidence that occupation authorities and courts often ignore their own procedural and substantive safeguards, including evidence of ownership, criteria for ownerless status, and ordinary requirements for clear identification of property in court decisions.[cite:267][cite:205][cite:282]

## Public Project and Repository Context

The public-facing project at [Mariupol Property Seizures — A Documented Evidence Base](https://amethyst-deceiver2001.github.io/Mariupol_Urbicide_2026/index.html) presents the property-seizure system as a documented “assembly line” in which utilities are cut off, homes are listed as “ownerless,” courts or administrations transfer them to municipal ownership, and the housing stock is then reallocated or redeveloped.[cite:205] The site states that this chain is supported by captured records, structured artifacts, and a source-hashed methodology designed to preserve evidentiary integrity.[cite:205] The associated GitHub repository, [Amethyst-Deceiver2001/Mariupol_Urbicide_2026](https://github.com/Amethyst-Deceiver2001/Mariupol_Urbicide_2026), is publicly presented as the backend for this forensic evidence base.[cite:205]

## Ukrainian Multi-Family Ownership Model

### Unit ownership and common property

Under Ukrainian law, individual apartments and non-residential premises in a multi-apartment building may be owned by natural persons, legal entities, territorial communities, or the state, but all such owners are co-owners of the building’s common property.[cite:251] Law No. 417-VIII “On the Peculiarities of Exercising Ownership in a Multi-Apartment Building” establishes that owners of apartments and non-residential premises are co-owners of the common property of the building, which includes structural and technical elements needed for the building’s functioning.[cite:242][cite:251][cite:253]

### Land under the building

A central feature of the Ukrainian model is that the land plot on which the building and its adjoining structures and territory are located is treated as part of the co-owners’ shared interest.[cite:247] The transitional provisions of Law 417-VIII, through amendments to Article 42 of the Land Code, provide for transfer of the land plot to the co-owners free of charge in ownership or permanent use under a Cabinet-approved procedure.[cite:247] The same framework expressly preserves co-owners’ rights to the land plot and adjoining territory if the building is destroyed.[cite:247]

### Management through co-owner institutions

Management is ordinarily exercised by the co-owners either directly, through a manager, or through an ОСББ (association of co-owners of a multi-apartment building).[^osbb] Kyiv municipal guidance and legal summaries describe the ОСББ as a legal entity created by owners to manage the common property and organize maintenance, without displacing the owners’ underlying title to their units, the common property, or the land.[cite:250][cite:243][cite:248]

[^osbb]: ОСББ = Об’єднання співвласників багатоквартирного будинку.

## Russian Property-Law Evolution Enabling Dispossession

### Crimea as proving ground: foreign owners as “foreigners” on their own land

Russia’s use of property law as an instrument of dispossession was developed first in occupied Crimea.[cite:237][cite:266] On 20 March 2020, Putin signed Decree No. 201 expanding the list of border territories in which foreign citizens and foreign legal entities may not own land, bringing most of occupied Crimea within this prohibition.[cite:258][cite:254] Because Russia treated Ukrainian citizens in occupied Crimea as foreign citizens for these purposes, Ukrainian landowners were compelled to alienate their plots within one year or face forced auction or transfer into state or municipal ownership.[cite:260][cite:265][cite:266]

This mattered beyond Crimea because it normalized two ideas: first, that Ukrainians on occupied territory can be legally reclassified as foreigners; and second, that large-scale land dispossession can be presented as an internal application of Russian property and border law rather than overt confiscation.[cite:263][cite:266] Rights groups and Ukrainian officials argued that this violated international law and property protections, but the mechanism itself became an operational precedent.[cite:256][cite:261]

### EGRN exclusivity and Federal Law 218-FZ

The next pillar was the elevation of the Russian Unified State Register of Real Estate (EGRN) into the sole accepted proof of ownership.[cite:233][cite:228] Mariupol municipal guidance states directly that a right registered in EGRN is the only proof of the existence of such right and that the presence of a recorded right in EGRN excludes recognition of the property as ownerless.[cite:228] This turns Russian registration from an evidentiary tool into a gatekeeping device: Ukrainian ownership records may exist, but if they have not been translated into Russian registration, the property becomes vulnerable to classification as ownerless.[cite:221][cite:228]

### Integration of occupied territories into Russian law

Russia’s annexation laws for occupied Donetsk, Luhansk, Zaporizhzhia, and Kherson provided the constitutional channel through which Russian civil, land, and registration law could be applied to occupied territory.[cite:259][cite:221] Municipal Mariupol guidance later tied the 1 July 2026 deadline for re-registration to amendments in the federal constitutional law governing DNR’s status within the Russian Federation.[cite:221] This made Russian law not just an occupation overlay but the internally asserted exclusive system for determining title, land rights, and municipal ownership.[cite:221][cite:259]

### 2025 federal law on “ownerless” housing

In late 2025, this architecture culminated in a specific federal law allowing residential buildings, apartments, and rooms in occupied territories that show signs of being ownerless property to be transferred to regional or municipal ownership.[cite:255][cite:259][cite:264] Reporting on the law indicates that Rosreestr and Rosimushchestvo coordinate with occupation bodies in defining and processing such housing.[cite:259] The law also authorizes subsequent allocation to categories such as local residents whose homes were lost, officials, military personnel, and other public employees.[cite:259][cite:264]

### Citizenship and personal appearance as barriers

Municipal Mariupol instructions make clear that owners with Russian citizenship may proceed through ordinary Russian administrative channels to register their homes, while owners without Russian citizenship must obtain special permission through the DNR property ministry and a collegial body.[cite:221] Owners abroad are told to act via consular power of attorney and representatives, but journalistic and rights reporting indicates that these formal routes are frequently obstructed or made practically unavailable.[cite:221][cite:235][cite:207] This creates a discriminatory hierarchy in which the ability to preserve housing depends not on underlying ownership but on passport status, access to occupied territory, and compatibility with Russian administrative demands.[cite:235][cite:221]

## How Mariupol Implements the System

### Municipal bodies and addresses

Mariupol’s occupation administration divides implementation between at least two key bodies.[cite:222][cite:231] The Управление имущественных и земельных отношений at **пер. Черноморский, 10** handles ownerless housing, commercial property with signs of ownerless status, social and commercial tenancy, and land-lease agreements.[cite:222][cite:225] The Управление жилищно-коммунального хозяйства at **бул. Шевченко, 301Б** maintains and publishes lists of dwellings with signs of ownerless status and receives owners who seek to remove their homes from those lists.[cite:231][cite:226]

### Procedure for owners and occupants

The official Mariupol guidance “Регистрация жилья с признаками бесхозяйности” lays out distinct pathways.[cite:221] Owners with Russian citizenship are told to register through a Russian MFC and Rosreestr; owners lacking Russian citizenship are directed to seek permission from a collegial body via the DNR ministry in Donetsk; non-owner occupants are routed to Chernomorsky 10 for identity checks, permissions, and possible later tenancy arrangements; and heirs are instructed to present proof of inheritance proceedings, in which case the administration says it will stop ownerless-property actions regarding the object.[cite:221]

### Repeated list publication and short deadlines

Mariupol’s official portals and associated local outlets repeatedly publish lists of housing with signs of ownerless status and tell owners to appear within 30 days with passport and title documents.[cite:226][cite:228][cite:224][cite:231] These rolling lists are paired with a citywide re-registration cutoff of 1 July 2026, creating a system in which delay, displacement, danger, or inability to comply on Russian terms can rapidly convert into municipal transfer.[cite:221][cite:207]

## Why Ukrainian Homeowners Are at a Structural Disadvantage

The core disadvantage is not only that Russia applies occupation law unlawfully, but that it substitutes a wholly different ownership logic.[cite:235][cite:257] Under Ukrainian law, displacement by war does not extinguish title, ownership is not conditioned on Russian citizenship, and co-owners retain rights to the land under and around their building even if the structure is destroyed.[cite:236][cite:247] Under the Russian occupation framework, by contrast, ownership becomes contingent on Russian-recognized paperwork, Russian registration, Russian citizenship or special permission, Russian deadlines, and dangerous physical interaction with occupation authorities.[cite:221][cite:228][cite:235]

This means that a Mariupol homeowner can remain a lawful owner under Ukrainian law while simultaneously being treated as an absent, foreign, or unregistered person under the Russian system, and their home as ownerless.[cite:236][cite:237] The conflict is not merely conceptual. It determines whether the property stays within a co-ownership structure tied to a surviving land right, or is pushed into a municipal pipeline that enables reassignment to officials, military personnel, or newcomers from Russia.[cite:247][cite:259][cite:235]

## Internal Contradictions and Apparent Violations of Russian Rules

Even within Russia’s own legal framework, available evidence suggests occupation authorities often disregard formal safeguards.[cite:267][cite:257] Human Rights Watch reviewed around 8,000 seizure cases across 25 occupied-territory courts and found repeated disregard of ownership evidence and owners’ efforts to assert rights, despite Russian-law rhetoric that only truly ownerless property should be transferred.[cite:267][cite:257] That pattern suggests that the legal process is frequently being used to ratify predetermined outcomes rather than to test whether statutory criteria are actually met.[cite:267]

The public Mariupol evidence base adds a second layer: it reports that none of the 2,694 published Mariupol ownerless rulings in its dataset contain street addresses, despite adjudicating title to immovable property, and that the broader DNR court corpus shows the same pattern.[cite:205] If accurate, that would represent a striking deviation from ordinary Russian civil practice, where the immovable object is normally identified precisely enough to be registered and enforced.[cite:205] Additional reporting indicates that confiscation also affects homes that are occupied, rebuilt, or otherwise do not obviously satisfy the stated criteria of abandonment or inability to identify the owner.[cite:282][cite:285]

## Conclusion

The evidence supports a coherent account of how mass dispossession in Mariupol became legally operable.[cite:205][cite:221][cite:259] Russian property law evolved from discriminatory foreign-ownership restrictions in Crimea to a broader system in which only Russian-recognized title, Russian citizenship status, Russian registration, and Russian administrative compliance preserve housing rights.[cite:258][cite:266][cite:235] This system directly collides with the Ukrainian multi-family ownership model, in which unit owners retain co-ownership of common property and the land under their building even after destruction, and it is implemented through municipal procedures that often appear to exceed or contradict even Russia’s own formal safeguards.[cite:247][cite:267][cite:205]
